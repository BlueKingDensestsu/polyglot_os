# THE 20 RULES OF FORMULATING KNOWLEDGE
## Dr. Piotr Wozniak (Creator of SuperMemo & Spaced Repetition)
### Adapted for Language Learning by Olivier's Polyglot OS

---

## RULE 1 — DO NOT LEARN WHAT YOU DO NOT UNDERSTAND

Before memorizing a word or grammar rule, you must understand it in context. If you're putting a German word into your palace and you don't understand the example sentence it appears in, that word will not stick. Understanding comes first. Memorization comes second. Always.

**In the app:** Grammar rule explanations appear before exercises. Palace words require example sentences. If you can't explain WHY a structure works, don't drill it yet — go back to input.

---

## RULE 2 — LEARN BEFORE YOU MEMORIZE

Build the big picture first, then drill the details. When you start a new language, spend the first 1-2 weeks just reading and listening — absorb the overall shape of the language before memorizing individual words or grammar rules. A word memorized without context is like a brick without a wall.

**In the app:** The Input-First Sprint Opening enforces this. No palace loading, no grammar drills for the first 1-2 weeks of a new language sprint. Input only.

---

## RULE 3 — BUILD UPON THE BASICS

Start simple. The shorter the initial chapter, the better. Simple models are easier to understand and you can always build complexity on top of them later. Don't skip the basics even if they seem obvious — you spend 50% of your review time on just 3-5% of your material. Basics take almost no time to maintain but cost you dearly if you forget them.

**In the app:** Vocabulary loads frequency-sorted — most common words fill the first palace landmarks. Grammar starts with the most fundamental structures.

---

## RULE 4 — STICK TO THE MINIMUM INFORMATION PRINCIPLE

The material you learn must be as simple as possible. Each card, each exercise, each retrieval should test ONE thing. A card that asks "What is the gender, meaning, and plural of Tisch?" is three questions pretending to be one. Split it. Simple items are easier to remember because your brain processes them the same way every time. Complex items create multiple neural paths that interfere with each other.

**In the app:** Palace Drill Mode tests individual aspects separately — meaning in one prompt, gender in another, usage in context in a third. Grammar exercises target one structure per sentence.

---

## RULE 5 — CLOZE DELETION IS EASY AND EFFECTIVE

A cloze deletion is a sentence with a missing piece: "Der Mechaniker ___ das Auto repariert." (hat). This format is fast to create and highly effective because the context sentence provides natural cues for recall. It's the backbone of fast learning.

**In the app:** Cloze is one of the five drill types in Palace Drill Mode. Grammar exercises use transformation (a harder version of cloze) for C1-level practice.

---

## RULE 6 — USE IMAGERY

Your visual cortex is vastly more powerful than your verbal processing. One picture is worth a thousand words — literally, in memory research. A graphic representation of information is far less volatile than text alone. If you can attach an image to a word, it will stick dramatically better.

**In the app:** Palace cards include images from Anki decks. For new languages, adding images during palace building is a priority. Google Image search in the target language (not English) gives culturally relevant associations.

---

## RULE 7 — USE MNEMONIC TECHNIQUES

The mental palace IS a mnemonic technique — the most powerful one known. But you don't need to force mnemonics on every word. With practice, you'll only consciously apply mnemonic techniques to 1-5% of your items. Most words stick from context and repetition. Reserve the elaborate palace associations for words that keep failing.

**In the app:** The entire palace system is built on the method of loci. Words marked as "Forgot" get priority for stronger mnemonic associations.

---

## RULE 8 — GRAPHIC DELETION IS AS GOOD AS CLOZE DELETION

Instead of removing a word from a sentence, you can remove a part from an image. Show a map with one country blank — name it. Show an anatomy diagram with one part covered — identify it. This works brilliantly for geography, anatomy, and any visual domain.

**In the app:** Potential future feature for languages with visual components (kanji recognition, Arabic letter forms in different positions).

---

## RULE 9 — AVOID SETS

Never make a card that asks "What are the dative prepositions?" — that's a set, and sets are nearly impossible to memorize reliably. Each time you review, you list them in a different order, leaving different neural traces that interfere with each other. Instead, learn each preposition individually in context. Convert sets into enumerations (ordered lists) or individual items.

**In the app:** Grammar exercises never ask "list all X." Each exercise targets one structure in one sentence. Palace entries are individual words, never grouped into categories.

---

## RULE 10 — AVOID ENUMERATIONS

Ordered lists are better than sets but still hard. If you must learn a sequence, use overlapping cloze deletions: "A ___ C D E", "B ___ D E F", "C ___ E F G" — each item is reinforced from multiple angles. But wherever possible, convert lists into meaningful individual facts connected by context.

**In the app:** Grammar chapter progressions are sequential but each exercise is independent. You never need to recite a list.

---

## RULE 11 — COMBAT INTERFERENCE

When you learn similar things, you confuse them. This is the single greatest cause of forgetting for experienced learners. "wurde" vs "würde", "ser" vs "estar", "は" vs "が" — these pairs actively destroy each other in memory. The only solution is to detect interference when it happens and create specific drills that force you to distinguish the confusing pair.

**In the app:** The Interference Detection Engine automatically flags pairs you consistently confuse. Discrimination drills present both items side by side and force binary choice, followed by production exercises.

---

## RULE 12 — OPTIMIZE WORDING

Every word in a prompt should earn its place. Shorter questions = faster retrieval = less confusion = better retention. If you can cut a word from a prompt without losing meaning, cut it. "PageMaker lost ground to ___" is better than "Aldus invented desktop publishing in 1985 with PageMaker. Aldus had little competition for years, and so failed to improve. Then Denver-based ___ blew past."

**In the app:** Grammar exercise prompts are concise. Export formats strip unnecessary context. Palace drill prompts are minimal.

---

## RULE 13 — PERSONALIZE AND PROVIDE EXAMPLES

A card asking "What is a soft bed without arms?" is worse than "What is a soft bed without arms? (like the one at Robert's parents)." Personal connections are incredibly resistant to interference and can reduce learning time dramatically. Link every word to your life, your memories, your experiences.

**In the app:** Palace words accept personal notes. When adding vocabulary manually, the app prompts for a personal association. The mental palace itself IS personalization — every landmark is a real place from your life.

---

## RULE 14 — RELY ON EMOTIONAL STATES

Shocking, funny, or emotionally vivid associations are retained dramatically better than neutral ones. Wozniak documents cases where adding one emotional example reduced forgetting by 25x over 5 years. The more absurd, bizarre, funny, or emotionally charged the mental image at a palace landmark, the better it sticks.

**In the app:** The palace system encourages vivid associations. "Schmetterling (butterfly) at the bakery because a giant butterfly is eating all the bread" sticks. "Schmetterling = butterfly" doesn't.

---

## RULE 15 — CONTEXT CUES SIMPLIFY WORDING

When your brain sees a specific visual context — a particular UI color, a landmark on your commute, a category label — it immediately narrows down which neural pathways to activate. This is why each language and each module in the app has a distinct visual identity. The German palace looks different from the Greek palace. The visual difference is a memory trigger, not decoration.

**In the app:** Per-language, per-module visual themes. Each language-module combination has its own distinct UI style. Switching languages switches the entire visual context.

---

## RULE 16 — REDUNDANCY DOES NOT CONTRADICT MINIMUM INFORMATION

Having multiple cards that test overlapping knowledge is not wasteful — it's strategic. Testing "Schmetterling → butterfly" AND "Der ___ flog über den Garten → Schmetterling" AND "butterfly in German → Schmetterling" reinforces the same word from three angles. The extra items strengthen the connection, not waste time.

**In the app:** Palace Drill Mode generates 3-5 prompts per word, each testing a different angle. This deliberate redundancy accelerates retention.

---

## RULE 17 — PROVIDE SOURCES

Know where your knowledge came from. If a grammar rule seems contradictory to something you learned before, the source tells you which to trust. In language learning, sources matter because textbooks sometimes disagree (especially on register and regional variation).

**In the app:** Vocabulary entries track their source (Anki, Lute, Language Reactor, manual). Grammar exercises reference their source chapter and book.

---

## RULE 18 — PROVIDE DATE STAMPING

Knowledge can become outdated. In language learning, this is less critical than in tech or medicine, but slang and cultural references do change. More importantly, date stamping your learning helps you see how long ago you learned something — if you learned a word 6 months ago and still remember it, it's solid.

**In the app:** Every vocabulary entry and exercise answer has a timestamp. The journal provides full date history. The gradient level system timestamps every assessment.

---

## RULE 19 — PRIORITIZE

You will always face more knowledge than you can master. Prioritize ruthlessly. In language learning, this means: high-frequency words before rare ones, common grammar structures before exotic ones, useful conversational phrases before literary vocabulary. Don't memorize the word for "ephemeral" before you know the word for "bread."

**In the app:** Vocabulary loads frequency-sorted. Grammar starts with the most commonly violated structures (identified by your error pattern tracker). The training plan generator prioritizes your weakest areas.

---

## RULE 20 — DO NOT NEGLECT UPDATING

As your knowledge grows, revisit old material. Your understanding of a grammar rule at B1 will be different from your understanding at C1. The same word you learned at A2 may have nuances you only appreciate at B2. Allow yourself to update, refine, and deepen existing knowledge rather than only adding new material.

**In the app:** The level-up test reassesses everything, not just new material. Palace review resurfaces old words. The gradient system (A1.1 → C1.5) ensures you keep building on foundations.

---

## SUMMARY: THE 5 MOST CRITICAL RULES FOR LANGUAGE LEARNING

If you remember nothing else, remember these:

**Rule 2:** Learn before you memorize. Input first, drilling second.

**Rule 4:** Minimum information. One retrieval per prompt. Split everything.

**Rule 6:** Use imagery. Pictures beat words every time.

**Rule 11:** Combat interference. Detect confusion pairs and drill them specifically.

**Rule 14:** Rely on emotion. Vivid, absurd, personal associations stick 25x better.
